Charger Safety Standards 2026: IEC 62368-1, UL 94 & Compliance Guide

Apr 12, 2026 10 min read Nina Nico

In the power adapter industry, safety isn't just a feature — it's a legal shield for your brand. With GaN chargers pushing higher wattages in smaller shells, understanding thermal and electrical safety has never been more critical. WOWOHCOOL breaks down the essential certifications for global markets. For a step-by-step walkthrough of US and EU certification requirements, see our US & EU charger certification guide.

Between 2025 and 2026, the US Consumer Product Safety Commission (CPSC) recalled over 447,000 chargers linked to fire and burn hazards — involving 112 incidents and 1 fatality. In a separate incident, HTRC and Haisito battery chargers were recalled after 33 reported fires and explosions affecting 4,800 units, causing 3 injuries and $224,000 in property damage. Approximately 40% of first-time certification submissions fail at least one critical safety test. Understanding charger safety standards is not optional — it is a market access requirement.

What safety certifications do I need to sell chargers in the US and EU? For the US: UL 62368-1 (safety) and FCC Part 15 (EMI). For the EU: CE marking including EN 62368-1 (safety) and EMC Directive 2014/30/EU. Additional standards apply: UL 2056 for power banks, IEC 62368-1 as global baseline, and UN38.3 for battery air transport. WOWOHCOOL holds all certifications across our product lines.

1. IEC 62368-1: The Global Safety Baseline

IEC 62368-1 has become the universal safety standard for audio/video and information technology equipment, replacing the older IEC 60950-1 and IEC 60065. All major markets now converge on this standard: UL 62368-1 (US), EN 62368-1 (EU), and CSA-C22.2 No. 62368-1 (Canada).

Important 2026 update: IEC 62368-1 Fourth Edition becomes mandatory on February 15, 2027 (US/International). A key change: components previously certified under IEC 60950-1 or IEC 60065 are no longer accepted — all components must now be certified to IEC 62368-1. New charger designs should target Fourth Edition compliance now to avoid rework.

Fire Prevention

IEC 62368-1 mandates stringent fire enclosure requirements. Materials must self-extinguish within defined timeframes. V-0 rated plastics (UL 94) self-extinguish in under 10 seconds with no flaming drips — exceeding the minimum V-1 requirement for most applications.

Electrical Shock Protection

Requirements for insulation, clearance, and creepage distances ensure accessible surfaces remain safe under fault conditions. Proper design maintains at least 4mm creepage for basic insulation and uses triple-insulated wire for transformer secondaries.

2. North America: UL & FCC Requirements

For the US market, UL 62368-1 is the de facto safety standard. While technically voluntary under federal law, UL certification is required by Amazon, Walmart, and Best Buy to list chargers. Without it, major retail channels are effectively closed.

UL 94 V-0 Fire-Retardant Materials

Charger housings must use materials that pass flame retardant testing:

  • — V-0 rating: Self-extinguishes in under 10 seconds, no flaming drips
  • — Critical for GaN chargers where high power density creates elevated thermal stress
  • — ETL mark (Intertek) is recognized as equivalent to UL by OSHA and major retailers

UL 94 7th Edition update (Feb 2026): New requirements apply to thin-wall enclosures (≤0.8mm wall thickness) — materials that previously passed V-1 may fail under the updated test methods. UL 94 markings must now include both the flame class (e.g., V-0) and the sample thickness. Chargers with ultra-slim designs common in GaN products should be re-verified against the 7th Edition standards.

FCC Part 15B

All chargers with switching power supplies must comply with FCC electromagnetic interference regulations. FCC has no mutual recognition with CE — separate testing is required.

3. European Union: CE & UKCA

Selling in the EU requires CE marking, which is a self-declaration process. The manufacturer must maintain a Technical File demonstrating compliance and is subject to market surveillance by national authorities.

CE-LVD (Low Voltage Directive)

Applies to products between 50-1000V AC or 75-1500V DC. Requires compliance with EN 62368-1, plus technical documentation including risk assessment and user safety instructions in local language.

ErP Directive (Energy Related Products)

Standby power must be below 0.3W. External power supplies must meet Level VI efficiency (average efficiency above 88%).

UK Post-Brexit: UKCA Marking

The UK requires UKCA marking instead of CE. CE is accepted during a transition period until 2027, but new product submissions should use UKCA.

Asia-Pacific Market Certifications

PSE (Japan), KC (South Korea), and CCC (China) are mandatory for their respective markets. WOWOHCOOL supports all three through established testing laboratory relationships.

5. Common Certification Failures

Understanding why chargers fail safety testing helps avoid costly redesigns. Approximately 40% of first-time submissions from new manufacturers fail at least one critical test.

1. Fire Enclosure Failures

The charger casing catches fire during fault condition testing. Common causes: insufficient flame-retardant materials, inadequate ventilation, or recycled plastics.

Solution: Specify V-0 rated UL94 materials that pass 850°C glow wire testing.

2. Temperature Limit Exceedance

Accessible surfaces exceed 77°C during normal operation, creating burn hazards.

Solution: Thermal pads, adequate heat sinks, OTP shutdown at 105°C.

3. Dielectric Strength Breakdown

Insulation fails under 1500V AC testing. Current leaks create shock hazards.

Solution: Maintain minimum 4mm creepage distances, use triple-insulated transformer wire.

4. Single Fault Conditions

When components fail, the charger creates hazards instead of safely shutting down.

Solution: Redundant protection circuits. Every safety function needs a backup.

5. Mechanical Strength Issues

Cracked housings, loose USB ports, or detached AC prongs under stress testing.

Solution: Ultrasonically welded seams, thermal glue for internal components.
