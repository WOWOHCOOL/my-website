1. GaN Generations at a Glance

Gallium Nitride (GaN) power semiconductors have evolved through multiple generations since their commercial introduction. Each generation brought measurable improvements in switching frequency, power density, thermal efficiency, and manufacturing cost — directly impacting what OEM buyers can achieve with their charger products.

The three generations you'll encounter in today's market are GaN I (first generation), GaN III (third generation), and GaN V (fifth generation). While GaN II and GaN IV exist in research contexts, GaN I, III, and V represent the commercial milestones that matter for OEM sourcing decisions.

2. GaN I — The Pioneer

GaN I emerged around 2018 as the first commercial GaN power semiconductor generation. It replaced silicon MOSFETs in select applications, offering switching frequencies around 1MHz — roughly 10x faster than silicon. This breakthrough allowed charger manufacturers to reduce transformer sizes and build smaller power adapters for the first time in decades.

Key characteristics: GaN I devices typically used a depletion-mode (d-mode) architecture, which required cascode configurations with silicon MOSFETs. This hybrid approach limited some of the theoretical advantages of pure GaN but still delivered meaningful size reductions — typically 20-30% smaller than equivalent silicon chargers at 45-65W power levels.

Today's relevance: GaN I is largely obsolete for new OEM designs. Most reputable manufacturers have moved to GaN III or GaN V. However, some budget-oriented chargers on the market may still use GaN I or even silicon labeled as "GaN" — a practice OEM buyers should watch for during supplier evaluation.

OEM note: If a supplier offers "GaN" charging at very low pricing without specifying the generation, there is a high probability they are using GaN I or lower-cost alternatives. Always request the specific GaN FET part number and datasheet during the quoting process.

3. GaN III — The Efficiency Leap

GaN III hit the market around 2020-2021, representing a significant advancement over the first generation. The key innovation was the shift to enhancement-mode (e-mode) architecture, eliminating the need for cascode silicon configurations. This allowed GaN III to operate at ~3MHz switching frequencies with reduced losses and improved thermal performance.

Key characteristics: GaN III offers approximately 30% size reduction over GaN I with peak efficiency reaching ~95%. Operating temperatures dropped to 75-80°C under full load, compared to 85-90°C for GaN I. Power delivery expanded to support PD 3.0 at up to 140W, making GaN III suitable for laptop charging and higher-power applications.

Today's relevance: GaN III remains widely used in mid-range charger products and OEM designs targeting cost-conscious market segments. Many "GaN chargers" on Amazon and in retail stores use GaN III. It delivers solid performance at a reasonable cost premium of 10-15% over GaN I solutions. For brands targeting mid-market pricing, GaN III OEM remains a viable option.

OEM note: GaN III is a good fit for brands launching their first GaN product line, as the lower BOM cost reduces financial risk. However, consider whether the 10-15% saving vs GaN V is worth sacrificing the marketing advantage of "5th Generation GaN" and the higher power capabilities.

4. GaN V — Today's Peak Performance

GaN V represents the current state of the art in commercial GaN power semiconductors. Available since 2023-2024, GaN V pushes switching frequencies beyond 5MHz while maintaining excellent thermal characteristics. The result: chargers that are approximately 50% smaller than GaN I equivalents, operate at 65-75°C under full load, and deliver PD 3.1 power up to 240W.

Key advantages for OEM buyers: GaN V's higher switching frequency enables significantly smaller magnetic components (transformers, inductors), which is the primary driver of size reduction. For a 100W charger, GaN V produces a product roughly 40% smaller than a GaN III equivalent and 50% smaller than GaN I. The 65-75°C operating temperature means cooler外壳, longer component life, and reduced risk of thermal-related returns.

6. How to Identify Real GaN V

The GaN charger market has a labeling problem. Some manufacturers label any GaN-based charger as "GaN V" regardless of the actual generation used. Here are practical verification methods for OEM buyers:

1. Check the Switching Frequency

Genuine GaN V operates at 5MHz or higher. Ask for the switching frequency specification in the datasheet. If a supplier cannot provide this number, it is a red flag.

2. Verify GaN FET Manufacturer

Real GaN V uses FETs from Navitas, Innoscience, GaN Systems, or EPC. Request the specific part number and verify it against the manufacturer's published specs.

3. Request Thermal Images

GaN V should maintain 65-75°C at full load. If the supplier's charger runs at 85°C+, it is likely using older generation GaN or enhanced silicon.

4. Compare PD 3.1 Capability

Only GaN V (and some advanced GaN III) supports PD 3.1 EPR at 140-240W. If a "GaN V" charger maxes out at 100W PD 3.0, it may not be genuine fifth generation.

5. Size Check

A genuine 100W GaN V charger is roughly the size of a traditional 30W silicon charger (approximately 70cm³ or less). If the product is significantly larger, it is not using GaN V.

7. Which Generation Should You Choose?

Your choice depends on your target market segment, pricing strategy, and brand positioning:

GaN I

Best for: Budget/economy lines

  • • Lowest BOM cost
  • • Adequate for basic 30-65W charging
  • • Limited marketing differentiation
  • • Medium return risk (heat-related)
GaN III

Best for: Mid-range / value

  • • Good value-performance balance
  • • Supports 65-140W PD 3.0
  • • Market as "GaN Technology"
  • • Lower heat-related returns
GaN V

Best for: Premium / flagship

  • ✓ Highest efficiency & lowest heat
  • ✓ PD 3.1 up to 240W
  • ✓ Market as "5th Gen GaN V"
  • ✓ Lowest return risk

For most B2B brands launching new charger products in 2026, GaN V is the recommended choice for flagship products while GaN III remains viable for mid-range offerings. The GaN V cost premium continues to shrink as production scales, and the marketing advantage of "5th Generation GaN V" technology provides clear shelf differentiation in an increasingly crowded market. If you are targeting Amazon, retail, or corporate procurement channels, GaN V's lower operating temperature and higher reliability translate directly to better reviews and fewer returns.

8. Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know what GaN generation a charger uses?

Check the datasheet for switching frequency (GaN V = 5MHz+), GaN FET part number, and supported PD version. Reputable OEM suppliers provide this information on request. If a supplier cannot specify the generation, assume it is GaN I or enhanced silicon.

Is GaN V worth the premium over GaN III?

For premium brand positioning: yes. The 20-35% BOM premium is offset by lower shipping weight (reduces freight cost), better thermal performance (fewer returns), and the ability to market "5th Generation GaN V" technology. The payback period is typically 6-12 months for most product launches.

Can GaN III support PD 3.1?

Some advanced GaN III designs support PD 3.1 up to 140W, but 240W EPR requires GaN V's higher switching frequency and thermal headroom.

What is the MOQ for OEM GaN V chargers?

500-1,000 units for standard ODM configurations with branding. Custom OEM designs with tooling: 2,000+ units. WOWOHCOOL offers lower MOQ for pilot runs.

Will GaN prices drop further?

Yes. GaN FET prices have been declining 10-15% annually as production scales. Industry analysts project GaN V will reach price parity with GaN III by 2027-2028, making now a good time to establish your GaN V product line ahead of mainstream adoption.
