1. What Is the Difference Between Cell and Rated Capacity?

The Real Capacity Formula

Don't trust the number on the box alone. Cell capacity and real output are different. Know this gap for accurate product specs and happy customers. Here is how to find real output:

(mAh × 3.7V × Efficiency) / Output V = Real Output

Example: A 10,000mAh power bank with 85% efficiency charging at 5V provides:

(10,000 × 3.7 × 0.85) / 5 = 6,290mAh

Capacity (mAh)

mAh measures the total energy storage of a power bank. This is the key spec for how many charges you get. More mAh means more charges but also more weight and size.

A 10,000mAh power bank gives 2-3 full phone charges in ideal conditions. Real-world results vary by device battery size, age, and charging conditions.

5,000mAh = 1-2 charges 10,000mAh = 2-3 charges 20,000mAh = 4-6 charges 26,800mAh = 6-8 charges

Input/Output Power (W)

Wattage (W) sets charging speed. The formula: Watts = Volts x Amps. An 18W power bank charges a phone much faster than a 5W one.

For fast charging, pick 18W, 30W, 45W, or 65W+ output. Higher wattage lets you charge laptops but creates more heat and costs more.

5W = Standard charging 18W = Fast charging (QC 3.0) 65W = Laptop charging

Ports & Connectivity

Modern power banks offer multiple port types: USB-A, USB-C, and Lightning input. USB-C with Power Delivery (PD) is the fastest and most versatile option, supporting bidirectional charging.

Multiple ports allow simultaneous charging of several devices. When using multiple ports, output power is typically distributed, reducing charging speed per device.

Conversion Rate

The actual usable capacity is 60-80% of the stated mAh due to energy loss during voltage conversion and heat generation. A 10,000mAh power bank typically delivers 6,000-8,000mAh to your device.

2. Which Battery Chemistry Is Better for Power Banks?

Know your battery types to pick between cost and performance. Both Li-ion and Li-polymer batteries power modern power banks. Each has its own traits.

Lithium-Ion (Li-ion)

  • Energy Density: 150-250 Wh/kg
  • Cost: Lower manufacturing cost
  • Shape: Cylindrical or prismatic cells
  • Lifespan: 500-1,000 charge cycles
  • Weight: Heavier per capacity unit

Best for: Budget-conscious buyers seeking high capacity at lower prices. Learn about our manufacturing process.

Lithium-Polymer (Li-Po)

  • Energy Density: 100-180 Wh/kg
  • Cost: Higher manufacturing cost
  • Shape: Flexible, custom form factors
  • Lifespan: 300-500 charge cycles
  • Weight: Lighter, ultra-thin designs

Best for: Slim power banks, premium devices, and applications requiring custom shapes.

Industry Data Point

According to Battery University, lithium batteries experience approximately 20-30% capacity degradation after 500 full charge cycles. This means a 10,000mAh power bank may only deliver 7,000-8,000mAh after 18 months of regular use.

3. Input & Output Protocols: PD, QC, and Beyond

Modern power banks use standard charging protocols. Know USB-C PD 3.1, PPS, and 140W laptop charging to avoid buying gear that won't work together.

Learn more: USB-C PD Fast Charging Guide →

USB-C Power Delivery (PD) 3.1

USB Power Delivery is the universal standard developed by the USB-IF organization. It supports power profiles from 15W to 240W across different voltage levels (5V, 9V, 15V, 20V, 28V).

PD 3.1 = Up to 240W PPS = Programmable Power Supply 140W = MacBook Pro Charging

Key advantage: Bi-directional charging allows using the power bank as both a charger and a device being charged through the same USB-C port.

Quick Charge (QC) by Qualcomm

Quick Charge technology, developed by Qualcomm, uses variable voltage (5V, 9V, 12V, 20V) to optimize charging speeds. The latest QC 5.0 delivers 100W+ charging with 70% improved efficiency over QC 4.0.

QC 5.0 = 100W+ output QC 4.0+ = USB PD compatibility

Other Notable Standards

  • Samsung AFC: Adaptive Fast Charging, proprietary to Samsung devices
  • Apple 20W PD: Apple's implementation of USB Power Delivery
  • VOOC/Warp Charge: OPPO/OnePlus proprietary fast charging (requires compatible cables)
  • PowerIQ: Anker's proprietary technology for device-optimized charging

CE (European Conformity)

Required for EU market. Ensures electromagnetic compatibility and electrical safety compliance.

FCC (Federal Communications Commission)

Mandatory for US market. Validates electromagnetic interference limits.

RoHS (Restriction of Hazardous Substances)

Independent testing for product safety. Particularly important for high-capacity power banks (20,000mAh+).

5. Calculate Power Bank Capacity for Your Needs

Pick the right capacity by knowing your device's battery size, use habits, and travel needs. This data-based method makes sure you never run out of power or spend too much on extra capacity.

Step 1: Know Your Device Battery Size

Device Type Typical Battery Notes
iPhone 15/16 3,349-4,422 mAh Varies by Pro model
Samsung Galaxy S24 4,000-5,000 mAh Ultra model has largest
Google Pixel 8 4,575-5,000 mAh Pro model larger
MacBook Air M3 52.6 Wh (~13,000 mAh) Requires 65W+ PD
iPad Pro 12.9" 40.88 Wh (~10,000 mAh) Supports 35W charging
Nintendo Switch 4,310 mAh ~2.5 charges from 10,000mAh

Step 2: Apply the Conversion Formula

Usable Capacity = Stated mAh × 0.70 (conversion rate) × (Power Bank Voltage / Device Voltage)

For example, a 10,000mAh power bank (3.7V) charging an iPhone 15 (3.87V battery):

10,000 × 0.70 × (3.7/3.87) = 6,699 mAh actual output

Step 3: Match Capacity to Use Case

Daily Commute

5,000-10,000mAh

1 full charge for most smartphones. Fits in jacket pockets. Lightweight at 150-250g.

Business Travel

10,000-20,000mAh

2-4 full charges. Charges laptop once. Fits in laptop bags. Weight: 300-450g.

Extended Travel

20,000-26,800mAh

4-6 full phone charges. Multiple device support. May require airline approval for 100Wh+.

Airline Regulations

According to IATA regulations, passengers may carry power banks up to 100Wh without airline approval. For 100-160Wh, airline approval is required. Anything above 160Wh is prohibited for passenger flights.

Quick math: A 26,800mAh power bank at 3.7V = 99.16Wh (within limit). A 30,000mAh = 111Wh (requires approval).

6. Common Power Bank Mistakes to Avoid

Based on industry data and user feedback, these are the most common buying and usage mistakes. They lead to poor results, safety risks, or wasted money. Avoid these to meet customer needs and safety rules.

01

Ignoring Real Capacity vs Stated Capacity

Many buyers assume 10,000mAh means 10,000mAh of usable power. In reality, conversion losses mean you only get 6,000-8,000mAh. Always calculate actual output using the conversion formula above.

02

Buying Based on Price Alone

Extremely cheap power banks often use recycled battery cells, inferior circuitry, and fake capacity ratings. According to Which? research, budget power banks under $15 frequently fail basic safety tests.

03

Mismatched Charging Standards

A power bank with QC 3.0 won't fast charge an iPhone (which uses PD). Similarly, VOOC requires specific cables. Verify your devices support the power bank's charging protocols before purchase.

04

Overlooking Input Power

Fast charging your phone means nothing if the power bank takes 12+ hours to recharge. Look for input power of 18W+ via USB-C PD for reasonable recharge times (4-6 hours for 10,000mAh).

05

Ignoring Watt-Hour Limits for Air Travel

Many travelers discover their 30,000mAh power bank is confiscated at security because it exceeds the 100Wh limit. Always check airline regulations before packing.

06

Using Damaged or Swollen Batteries

Signs of battery swelling (warped casing, difficulty inserting into devices) indicate dangerous chemical reactions. Immediately discontinue use and dispose properly at certified e-waste facilities.
